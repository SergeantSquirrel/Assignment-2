1.1 Introduction

Machine learning is an evolving branch of computational algorithms that are designed to emulate human intelligence by learning from the surrounding environment (El Naqa I. et al. 2015). It is generally referred to as the most popular technologies in the fourth industrial revolution and usually enables systems to enhance and learn from experience automatically without being specifically programmed (Sarker, I.H., Kayes, A.S.M. & Badsha, S. et al 2020).

In general, the effectiveness and the efficiency of a machine learning solution depend on the performance of the learning algorithms and the characteristics and nature of data. There are various forms of data such as structured, semi-structured, or unstructured (Han J., Pei J. & Kamber M. 2011). Different types of machine learning techniques are used to analyse such data in a particular problem domain, and to extract the useful knowledge or insights from the data to develop real-world intelligent applications according to their learning capabilities.


1.2 Algorithms, Techniques and Data Structures

Machine Learning algorithms are mainly divided into four categories: Supervised learning, Unsupervised learning, Semi-supervised learning, and Reinforcement learning.

  • Supervised learning which is also known as classification is typically the task of machine learning to learn a function that maps an input to an output based on sample input-output pairs (Han J., Pei J. & Kamber M. 2011).
  • Unsupervised learning which is also known as clustering analyses unlabelled datasets without the need for human interference, i.e., a data-driven process (Han J., Pei J. & Kamber M. 2011).
  • Semi-supervised learning can be defined as a hybridization of the above-mentioned supervised and unsupervised methods, as it operates on both labelled and unlabelled data(Han J., Pei J. & Kamber M. 2011).
  • Reinforcement learning enables software agents and machines to automatically evaluate the optimal behaviour in a particular context or environment to improve its efficiency (Kaelbling L.P., Littman M.L. & Moore A.W. 1996).

1.3 Application Areas

In the following, we will discuss three popular application areas of machine learning technology.

  • Predictive analytics and intelligent decision-making: The basis of predictive analytics is capturing and exploiting relationships between explanatory variables and predicted variables from previous events to predict the unknown outcome (Han J., Pei J. & Kamber M. 2011). They can improve any decision-making sectors since accurate predictions provide insight into the unknown.
  • Cybersecurity and threat intelligence: Machine learning has become a crucial cybersecurity technology that constantly learns by analysing data to identify patterns, which allows detection of malware and suspicious activities online.
  • Healthcare: Machine learning can help to solve diagnostic and prognostic problems in a variety of medical domains, such as disease prediction, medical knowledge extraction, detecting regularities in data, patient management, etc. (Fatima M. & Pasha M. 2017). For instance, learning techniques can be used to tackle pandemics by classifying high risk patients, mortality rate, outbreak prediction, and ideal treatment which contribute to early responses.

1.4 Future Trends

Machine learning development is challenging even for experienced researchers due to its complexity and the need of proper evaluation in order to achieve accurate results. Indeed Arbabshirani et al. showed in a recent review of ML studies in the area of neuroimaging that 73% of studies employed a single machine (namely the Support Vector Machine) and almost no study optimized hyperparameters even for that single machine. This indicates that no machine learning algorithm will overall perform better than any other without further knowledge of data generating process. Against this background, we see increasing efforts to automatize the entire ML development process. Examples for this include auto-sklearn (a framework focused on machine selection and Bayesian hyperparameter optimization), PHOTON (a package enabling cross-platform ML pipeline construction, optimization, and evaluation), and Auto Keras (an open-source package based on TensorFlow for neural network architecture search) (Cearns, M., Hahn, T. & Baune, B.T. 2019).

Besides that, telecommunications are also one of the future trends which machine learning should be integrated with. Increasing network traffic posts serious problems as multiple devices would become part of a network with the arrival of 5G technology in telecommunications. In this context, massive or large Multiple-Input Multiple-Output (MIMO) is an essential element to increase spectral efficiency in 5G by exploiting the spatial degrees of freedom of multiplexing messages for several users in the same frequency, or to focus the radiated signal toward intended receivers to minimize intra- and inter-cell interference (F. Rusek et al. 2013). Contributions of ML in MIMO technology includes channel estimation and direction of arrival (DOA) estimation by using deep neural networks (DNN) to learn the statistics of the wireless channel and the spatial structures in the angle domain (H. Huang et al. 2018). Furthermore, ML can contribute to MIMO through the classification of the channel state information (CSI) to select the optimal antenna indices using supervised learning.


2.1 Social Impact

Machine learning is an important component of the growing field of data science. Algorithms are trained to make predictions or classifications, uncovering key insights within data mining projects through the use of statistical methods. These insights eventually promote decision making within businesses and applications, ideally affecting key growth metrics. The market demand for data scientists will increase as big data continues to grow and expand, requiring them to identify the most relevant business questions and eventually the data to answer them. However, implementing machine learning within businesses has indeed raised many ethical concerns surrounding AI technologies. One clear example is it leads to data privacy issues. As machine learning involves collecting and analysing a large dataset, it can pose serious negative consequences in leaking personal information without proper measurements which leads to social crimes such as identity theft.


2.2 Impact on Employment and job

Any form of technological development, and in particular digitalisation, has major implications for labour markets. Assessing its impact will be crucial for developing policies that promote efficient labour markets for the benefit of workers, employers and societies as a whole (Neufeind, M, Reilly, J & Ranft, F 2013). There are two main ways where machine learning can affect employment, either by increasing the demand for labour in jobs or industries that arise from technological progress (productivity effect) or by directly replacing workers from tasks that were performed more effectively by using machine learning technologies (displacement effect). In general, the demand for jobs that require specific skills and routine tasks will decline as machine learning technologies ought to outperform human brain power in those sectors. On the other hand, despite machine learning being reframed as causing job loss, it is undeniable that the market demand for specific job roles shifts with the introduction of disruptive and new technology. For example, the introduction of machine learning in language translating led to a decline in interpreter jobs, but new industries also emerged, with a net positive impact on employment. The manufactory of the new technology devices industry itself grew fast, creating many new jobs along the way.


3 Expectations of Contribution in Daily Life

One of the most notable impacts of machine learning on my daily life will be contributing to personalized digital media. In terms of personalization, most legacy media are currently testing or launching recommender systems which collect all the past behaviour, interaction and web searches in order to tailor the ideal experience for us (Ignatidou S. 2019).

From notifications that we receive from social media apps (Facebook, Instagram, Twitter), feeds that we see on the timeline, recommendations on video streaming apps (Youtube, Netflix), and pre-created playlists on music streaming services such as Spotify, machine learning continues to reward me with a better online experience by increasing efficiency while saving cost and time.

Integration of machine learning in smart homes and home security systems contribute to increasing my family's quality of life every day. For instance, integrated alarm systems and surveillance cameras enable residents to detect potential intruders and take early precaution measures to protect home security. Besides that, machine learning contributes to development of facial recognition technology that can build a catalogue of frequent visitors at home and recognizes unusual visitors. Digital assistants like Amazon Echo and Alexa allow for voice-activated control of our smart home such as dimming light, locking the door, etc at our command.

Through the appropriate application of machine learning to increasingly available electronic health data, a more advanced and mature healthcare system can be achieved. By means, family and friends can receive ideal medication through faster patient diagnosis. Good health can be maintained and symptoms can be discovered early through regular medical check-up.

Besides that, machine learning is the future of every business. My parents own a retail business and the business profit highly relies on statistics and data. Machine learning enables an analysis of a massive quantity of data and can provide a faster and more accurate result that can help in identifying profitable opportunities and dangerous risks. It cuts down labour, cost and time needed to collect and analyse marketing data which means more time can be spent with family.
